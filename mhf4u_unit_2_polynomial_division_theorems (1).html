The Transition: The "Monster"

You can easily sketch graphs when they are given in Factored Form.

But what if a test gives you THIS standard form equation?

f(x) = 2x3 - 3x2 - 3x + 2
  • You can't see the x-intercepts.
  • You can't factor it using Grade 10 quadratic rules.
  • We must learn how to Divide it.

Exam Trap: Missing Placeholders

Divide (x3 - 8) by (x - 2)

x3 + 0x2 + 0x - 8

If a degree is skipped in the original polynomial, you MUST insert a zero as a placeholder.
Otherwise, your subtraction columns will not line up!

Synthetic Division: The "Cheat Code"

Long division works, but it takes up half a page. Enter Synthetic Division.

  • It completely removes the variables (x) and only looks at the coefficients.
  • Rule 1: Only use this when dividing by a linear factor like (x - k).
  • Rule 2: Put the ROOT outside the bracket, not the factor.

The Remainder Theorem

What if a question asks for the remainder, but you don't need the quotient?

When a polynomial P(x) is divided by (x - k),

the remainder is exactly equal to P(k).

Method 1: Division

Divide (x3 - 4x2 + 5x - 1) by (x - 2)

Remainder = 1

Method 2: The Theorem (Faster)

Evaluate P(2)

P(2) = (2)3 - 4(2)2 + 5(2) - 1
P(2) = 8 - 16 + 10 - 1 = 1

The Factor Theorem

Think about normal numbers. If 10 ÷ 5 has a remainder of 0, then 5 is a perfect factor.

If P(k) = 0,
then (x - k) is a FACTOR.

This is how we find the first piece of the puzzle when breaking down a standard form equation!

The Boss Fight: Connect it All

Fully factor the polynomial: f(x) = x3 - 7x - 6

Step 1: Guess & Check (Factor Theorem)

Test factors of the constant (-6): ±1, ±2, ±3, ±6

f(-1) = (-1)3 - 7(-1) - 6 = 0

→ (x+1) is a factor!

Step 2: Synthetic Division

Divide f(x) by (x+1) to find the remaining quadratic.

(Hint: Remember the 0x2 placeholder!)

Quotient = x2 - x - 6

Step 3: Factor the Quadratic

f(x) = (x + 1)(x2 - x - 6)
f(x) = (x + 1)(x - 3)(x + 2)
